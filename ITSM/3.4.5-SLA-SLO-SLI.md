# **SLAs, SLIs, and SLOs in Production Support**

## **Learning Objectives**

By the end of this module, learners will be able to:

*   Define **SLA, SLI, and SLO** and explain the differences
*   Understand how they are used in real-world production environments
*   Identify appropriate metrics for reliability and performance
*   Apply concepts to incident management, monitoring, and reporting
*   Understand the impact of breaches and accountability

***

# **1. Start with the Big Picture: Reliability in Production Support**

In production support, your primary goal is **keeping systems reliable and usable**.

Reliability is not abstract—it is defined using measurable commitments:

*   **What are we measuring? → SLI (Indicator)**
*   **What is our target? → SLO (Objective)**
*   **What have we promised customers? → SLA (Agreement)**

Think of these as a **layered model**:

    SLI (metrics) ➜ SLO (targets) ➜ SLA (contract/commitment)

***

# **2. Service Level Indicator (SLI)**

## **What is an SLI?**

An **SLI is a quantitative measurement of system performance or reliability.**

It answers:

> “How well is the service actually performing right now?”

## **Common SLIs in Production Support**

*   **Availability (uptime)**  
    e.g., “Service is available 99.5% of the time”
*   **Latency (response time)**  
    e.g., “API responds within 200ms”
*   **Error rate**  
    e.g., “<1% of requests fail”
*   **Throughput**  
    e.g., “Processes 500 transactions/sec”

## **Example**

A payments API:

*   10,000 requests/minute
*   50 failed requests

<!---->

    Error rate SLI = 50 / 10,000 = 0.5%

## **Why SLIs Matter**

*   Provide **objective visibility** into system health
*   Feed monitoring dashboards and alerts
*   Enable data-driven decisions during incidents

## **Production Support Context**

SLIs are what on-call engineers watch during:

*   Incident triage
*   Root cause analysis
*   Post-incident reviews

***

# **3. Service Level Objective (SLO)**

## **What is an SLO?**

An **SLO is a target or goal for an SLI.**

It answers:

> “What level of performance are we aiming for?”

## **Examples**

*   Availability SLO: **99.9% uptime**
*   Latency SLO: **95% of requests < 300ms**
*   Error rate SLO: **<1% failures**

## **Error Budget Concept**

A key SRE principle:

    Error Budget = 100% - SLO

Example:

*   SLO = 99.9% uptime
*   Error Budget = 0.1% downtime

This defines:

*   Acceptable failure
*   Risk tolerance
*   Release velocity

## **Why SLOs Matter**

*   Align engineering and business priorities
*   Prevent over-engineering (100% uptime is unrealistic)
*   Help balance innovation vs reliability

## **Production Support Context**

SLOs guide:

*   Alert thresholds (when to wake someone up)
*   Prioritization during incidents
*   Decisions to **halt releases** if reliability drops

***

# **4. Service Level Agreement (SLA)**

## **What is an SLA?**

An **SLA is a formal, customer-facing agreement that defines expected service performance and consequences for failure.**

It answers:

> “What have we promised customers or stakeholders?”

## **Examples**

*   “Service will be available **99.9% monthly uptime**”
*   “Critical incidents will receive a response within **15 minutes**”
*   “Refunds/credits issued if SLA is not met”

## **Key Components of an SLA**

*   Performance targets (often based on SLOs)
*   Measurement methodology
*   Reporting frequency
*   Penalties or service credits

## **Why SLAs Matter**

*   Set **customer expectations**
*   Build trust
*   Create legal/financial accountability

## **Production Support Context**

When SLAs are breached:

*   Customers may escalate incidents
*   Business impact increases
*   Financial penalties may occur
*   Leadership visibility increases

***

# **5. Key Differences (Important for Learners)**

| Aspect      | SLI                     | SLO               | SLA                            |
| ----------- | ----------------------- | ----------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Definition  | Measurement             | Target            | Agreement                      |
| Audience    | Internal                | Internal          | External                       |
| Purpose     | Track performance       | Set goals         | Set commitments                |
| Example     | “Response time = 250ms” | “95% < 300ms”     | “Customers guaranteed < 500ms” |
| Enforcement | Monitoring              | Alerts & policies | Legal/financial                |

### **Simple Analogy**

*   **SLI** = Your speedometer (current speed)
*   **SLO** = Speed limit (target)
*   **SLA** = Contract saying you’ll be fined if you break the limit

***

# **6. How They Work Together (Real Example)**

## **Scenario: E-commerce Website**

### Step 1: Define SLIs

*   Availability
*   Checkout response time
*   Payment success rate

### Step 2: Set SLOs

*   99.9% uptime
*   95% of checkout responses < 2 seconds
*   99% successful payments

### Step 3: Define SLA

*   “99.5% uptime guaranteed monthly”
*   “Credits issued if uptime < 99.5%”

### Key Insight:

> **SLOs are stricter than SLAs**  
> This creates a safety buffer.

## **Example Dashboard**

*   Error Rate: 0.7%

# **8. Alerting and Incident Response**

## **SLO-Based Alerting**

Instead of alerting on raw metrics:

*   Alert when **SLO is at risk**

Example:

*   Alert if error rate >2% for 5 minutes
*   Trigger on-call escalation

## **Why This Matters**

*   Reduces alert fatigue
*   Focuses attention on **user-impacting issues**

***

# **9. What Happens When They Are Breached?**

## **If SLI Degrades**

*   Monitoring detects abnormal values
*   Alerts triggered

## **If SLO is Breached**

*   Incident declared
*   Root cause analysis initiated
*   Release freeze may occur
*   Error budget consumed

## **If SLA is Breached**

*   Customer notifications
*   Financial penalties or credits
*   Escalations to leadership
*   Potential reputational damage

***

# **10. Instructor Tip: Common Learner Confusions**

### Confusion #1: “SLO and SLA are the same”

Clarification:

*   SLO = internal goal
*   SLA = external promise

***

### Confusion #2: “Higher SLO is always better”

Clarification:

*   Overly aggressive SLOs:
    *   Increase burnout
    *   Slow innovation
    *   Are often unrealistic

***

### Confusion #3: “SLIs are just metrics”

Clarification:

*   Not all metrics are SLIs
*   SLIs must reflect **customer experience**

# **12. Key Takeaways**

*   **SLI** = What you measure
*   **SLO** = What you aim for
*   **SLA** = What you promise

In production support:

*   SLIs drive observability
*   SLOs guide operations and engineering trade-offs
*   SLAs protect customer expectations and business commitments

***

# **Final Thought for Learners**

> “You don’t improve reliability by guessing—you improve it by measuring (SLIs), targeting (SLOs), and committing (SLAs).”
